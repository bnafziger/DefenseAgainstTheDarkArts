COMP 116: Introduction to Computer Security, Fall 2015

The Final Project

Absolute Deadline: Tuesday, December 15th at 6 PM

Overview

We have covered a plethora of security topics in this class. For the final project in the class, you will delve into a current security issue of your choice. This is not only your opportunity to specialize on a topic, but also to stand out and gain hands-on experience in the field.

History

There is a rich history of past projects in this class including Network Anonymity Through “MAC Swapping” by Andrew Sayler (spring 2011); published in Volume 28, Issue 3 (Autumn 2011) of 2600: The Hacker Quarterly. An archive of past final projects is available at http://www.cs.tufts.edu/comp/116/archive/.

Outcomes

My hope of this final project is that you will:

  1. Specialize in a current security issue of your choice
  2. Gain hands-on experience in the field
  3. Demonstrate technical knowledge
  4. Communicate clearly to a community

Instructions

For this final project, you must:

  1. Choose a mentor
  2. Write a paper
  3. Provide your own supporting material that complements your paper

The Mentor

The mentor can either me or someone in the industry. The mentor cannot be a student in the class. Should you choose someone other than me as your mentor, please send me the person's name, title, and e-mail address.

The Paper

The following are the guidelines for the paper:

  • Required sections of paper:
    • Abstract
    • Introduction
    • To the Community - Why did you choose this topic? Why is this important to know?
    • One of the following:
      • Action Items - In your own words, what do people need to do about the subject matter?
      • Defenses - Recommended if you work is attack-oriented. In your own words, how do you defend against the subject matter?
      • Applications - Recommended if your work is more theoretical. In your own words, how can you apply your subject matter to the real world?
    • Conclusion or Summary
    • References - You must use at least five (5) high-quality references. If you cite Wikipedia as a reference, it will result in a 25 point penalty!

The Supporting Material

Along with your paper, you must produce a supporting material for your paper. Options:

  1. Source code: This can be a demo, an exploit, a new security tool, an extension to an existing security tool or product, a script, a proof-of-concept, or an analysis of the source code. You can use any programming language and there is no requirement for lines-of-code that you have to write. Any source code that you write must be made public on GitHub
  2. A video: Must be at least five (5) minutes long.

IMPORTANT: This supporting material must be made publicly available. That is, private submissions will not be accepted.

Ideas

  • Security visualization
  • Modern malware
  • Advanced cryptography
  • Analyzing life safety and medical devices
  • Secure communications
  • Software Defined Radio (SDR)
  • Any collaborative cyber effort with government (all levels), International Relations
  • See https://www.defcon.org/html/defcon-22/dc-22-cfp.html
  • See https://www.defcon.org/html/defcon-23/dc-23-cfp.html
  • Some junk hacking

Unacceptable Ideas

  • Some junk hacking
  • Modding a video game console
  • Anything pertaining to glitches
  • Securing WordPress

Grading

Your final project will be graded on the following:

  • Paper (75%)
    1. Abstract (5%; see below)
    2. Outline (5%; see below)
    3. Content and structure
    4. Language
    5. References
    6. Knowledge and wisdom
    7. Community service
    8. State-of-the-art
  • Supporting Material (25%)

See http://www.giac.org/media/certifications/gold/gold_rubric.pdf for the rubric I will use for grading the paper
